# Word choice (Michael)

Use this reference for vocabulary level, formality, contractions, jargon, favourite words, and avoided patterns. All examples are from the analysed posts.

## Vocabulary level

**Moderate to sophisticated.** Technical posts use precise domain terms without dumbing down; explanatory prose stays in plain, concrete language.

- **Technical (when audience is technical):** *vtable*, *provenance*, *amortised*, *RAII*, *cognitive load*, *pit of success*.
- **Plain when explaining:** *"The idea is you create a struct which will act as an 'abstract base class'"*; *"Seeing something visualised is often enough to summon the correct keywords"*.
- **Sophisticated but readable:** *"ostracise"*, *"unpack"* as a verb for breaking down complexity, *"encapsulating"*, *"conspiratorial aside"*.
- **Deliberate simplicity for non-programmers:** *"we figure out a bunch of possible paths then use clever algorithms employed in the games industry to find the best overall route"* instead of naming A*.

**Quoted examples:**

- *"This creates content that serves the writer's ego more than the reader's needs. I mean, haven't we all been there?"*
- *"When you assume confusion indicates explanation failure rather than reader failure, you start thinking about problems differently."*
- *"The ability to correctly communicate an idea or concept in a way that others can understand (i.e. to name things) is by far the most important. It's also really hard."*
- *"After all, the simplest code is no code."*

## Formality

**Scale: 2–2.5 / 5 (casual to slightly informal).**

- **Direct address:** Consistent *"you"* and *"we"*. *"You can expect direct, honest feedback"*; *"When you need to explain something complex, resist the urge to build it up piece by piece"*.
- **First person:** *"I"* for experience and stance. *"I'm someone who's productivity levels change wildly depending on my motivation levels"*; *"I eventually got quite good at this"*.
- **Conversational openers:** *"Here's the thing"*, *"Let's be honest"*, *"I mean"*, *"Don't get me wrong"*.
- **No corporate or stiff phrasing:** Avoids *"one might consider"*, *"it is important to note"*. Prefers *"It's worth noting"*, *"Something to keep in mind"*, *"The key is"*.

**Quoted examples:**

- *"I mean, haven't we all been there? You're trying to solve a problem, you find what looks like the perfect article, and five minutes later you're somehow more confused than when you started."*
- *"Don't get me wrong, sometimes there are situations where people who know just enough to be dangerous will say 'that's really complex, why can't you just do X?'"*
- *"So yeah, it was a hard decision to leave because I loved my teammates and the project, but I'm confident it was the right one."*

---

## Contractions

**Usage: Always / very frequent.** Contractions are the default. Full forms appear mainly for emphasis or in short, punchy lines.

**Representative:** *"I've been writing technical content for years, and I'll be honest"*; *"There's quite a lot going on here, so let's unpack it a bit"*; *"Don't think of these as hard and fast rules"*; *"We'll need to add the \`cc\` crate as a dependency"*; *"That's one of the things I really like about the language"*.

**Rare full forms (for emphasis or rhythm):** *"It is important to ask the question"*; *"It's important to note"* (when stating the important thing).

---

## Jargon

**Industry terms when audience is technical; avoided or explained when not.**

- **Technical posts:** Standard terms (*trait object*, *vtable*, *unsafe*, *ABI*, *repr(C)*, *dynamic dispatch*) with links or short clarifications.
- **Anti-jargon stance:** Avoid *"navmesh"* and *"A* pathfinding algorithm"* with non-programmers; prefer *"we figure out a bunch of possible paths then use clever algorithms employed in the games industry to find the best overall route"*.
- **Jargon introduced then unpacked:** *"Cognitive load management"*, *"Context Hook"*, *"Unpack technique"* are named then explained in plain language.

**Quoted examples:**

- *"Using opaque words like 'web server', 'Event-Driven Architecture', or 'Apache Kafka' ... a great way to ostracise and confuse people"*
- *"This isn't just about being nice to readers (though it is that too). It's about taking responsibility for communication success."*

---

## Strong language

**Level: None. Style: Direct and sometimes blunt, never crude.**

- No profanity in the sampled posts.
- **Blunt but professional:** *"I'm going to be brutal here"*; *"Don't be that guy"*; *"that sort of turnover is *insane*"*.
- **Mild intensifiers:** *"really"*, *"pretty"*, *"kinda"*, *"quite"*, *"a bit"* for tone, not aggression.

## Avoided words / patterns (word choice)

- **Overly formal hedges:** Avoid *"it is noteworthy that"*, *"one should consider"*, *"it may be said that"*. Prefer *"It's worth noting"*, *"Something to keep in mind"*, or direct assertion.
- **Filler intensifiers:** Doesn't overuse *"very"*, *"extremely"*, *"incredibly"*. Prefers *"really"*, *"quite"*, *"pretty"* in moderation.
- **Passive without reason:** Prefers active voice; passive when the actor is irrelevant.
- **Corporate buzzwords:** No *"leverage"*, *"synergy"*, *"bandwidth"* (non-technical sense), *"circle back"*, *"move the needle"*.

**Signature sentences (word-choice flavour):** *"There's quite a lot going on here, so let's unpack it a bit"*; *"After all, the simplest code is no code"*; *"Don't be that guy"*; *"I mean, haven't we all been there?"*; *"Feel free to browse through and steal code or inspiration"*; *"More often than not *if it compiles, it works*"*; *"9 times out of 10..."*.
