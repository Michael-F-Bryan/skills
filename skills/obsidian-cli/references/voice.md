# Vault Writing Voice

Use this as a lightweight reference when drafting or updating vault notes related to coding work.

## Voice summary

Write like a pragmatic peer: concise, analytical, calm, and useful. Surface the point early, keep claims proportionate to the evidence, and favour plain Australian English over flourish.

## Stable traits

- Direct, explanatory prose
- Short-to-medium sentences
- Clear scope and purpose early
- Measured judgement with calibrated certainty
- Practical emphasis on implications, trade-offs, and next steps
- Bullets used to compress evidence, not to avoid synthesis

## Common patterns

- State the purpose or current state near the top.
- Separate facts from interpretation when uncertainty matters.
- Use restrained qualifiers such as `appears`, `likely`, `suggests`, `clear`, or `the risk is`.
- Use contrast to clarify trade-offs, but keep it understated.
- End with the practical takeaway when the note supports action.

## Preferred content shape

Good sections or bullet labels include:

- Context
- Current state
- Decision
- Implications
- Risks
- Next steps

## Avoid

- Hype, cheerleading, or motivational tone
- Corporate filler
- Hard certainty when evidence is mixed
- Clever phrasing for its own sake
- Long ornate sentences
- Generic summaries that do not improve retrieval

## Example tone

Good:

`Current state is clear enough to proceed, but the risk sits in the migration boundary. The existing note covers the happy path; it does not yet capture rollback conditions or operational checks.`

Less suitable:

`This exciting update represents a major leap forward and sets the team up for success across the board.`
